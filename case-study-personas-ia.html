The Problem

Built by engineers, for no one in particular

ThirdPartyTrust had been running for years before a UX/UI team existed. It was built by engineers — solid technically, but without a user-centered design process. Two fundamentally different users — customers (companies managing third-party risk) and vendors (companies being assessed) — were navigating the same interface, even though their goals, workflows, and mental models had almost nothing in common.

Without a UX team, there was no user feedback infrastructure in place. Companies without a subscription were generating a high volume of support tickets with no clear signal about what was actually failing. There was no customer success support for them either — just noise, and no way to make sense of it.

Companies with no subscription had a significant amount of issues and tickets, with no clear user feedback.

When I joined ThirdPartyTrust, the signal was already there. The job was to build the process to read it — and then act on it.

Research Approach

Mixed methods, deliberate choices

The core challenge was understanding two very different user populations with different goals, contexts, and levels of platform access.

Persona Research + Interviews

To understand motivations, mental models, and workflows for each user type. The goal was exploratory — we didn't know what we didn't know.

Affinity Diagramming

To synthesize observations across participants and give VRM and TMH teams a shared space to build common understanding — not defend assumptions.

Usability Studies

To validate new workflows once the proposed IA was defined — testing whether the new structure matched how users actually wanted to navigate.

Participant Strategy: Primary vs. Secondary

Not all users had equal weight. Personas give focus rather than coverage — so I defined 2 primary and 2 secondary personas based on subscription status, engagement level, and research signal strength.

Emotional Arc
Key Takeaways From Research

Five themes. Five design principles.

The affinity diagramming sessions consolidated findings into five core themes — each one became a design principle for the new IA and product experience.

01
Time Saving

Customers were running risk programs manually — spreadsheets, email chains, follow-ups. Vendors were copy-pasting the same answers repeatedly.

Workflows had to be fast, linear, and forgiving. No dead ends. No unnecessary steps.
02
Customization

Neither customers nor vendors experienced the platform as built for their specific context. Both needed control — customers over risk criteria, vendors over their profile.

Customization had to be a first-class concept — not a buried settings page.
03
Collaboration

Both sides described their work as inherently collaborative — customers with internal security teams, vendors coordinating across legal, engineering, and finance.

Contacts, roles, and permissions needed to be core navigation objects — not nested utilities.
04
Automation

Repetitive manual tasks — assigning questionnaires one by one, chasing vendors, tracking progress in external spreadsheets — were the biggest source of friction.

Automation touchpoints needed to be integrated into primary flows, not hidden away.
05
Navigation

Users couldn't orient themselves. The shared UI meant a customer looking for their vendor list and a vendor looking for their active assessments ended up in the same place with no clear path. Navigation was the symptom of a deeper structural problem: the platform had no conceptual model that matched how either user type thought about their work.

The IA itself needed to be rebuilt around user mental models — which is where the object-oriented design approach became critical.
From Research to Architecture

Objects first, not features

The research revealed that customers and vendors weren't just different personas — they were operating with fundamentally different conceptual objects. Putting them in the same navigation was the root cause of the confusion.

Customer (VRM) — Core Objects

Vendor
The company being assessed
Requirement
A questionnaire, certification, or audit assigned to a vendor
Assessment
The active evaluation lifecycle
Finding
A risk identified during assessment

Vendor (TMH) — Core Objects

Profile
Their security posture, presented to customers
Request
An incoming requirement — questionnaire, certification, or insurance
Certificate
SOC 2, ISO 27001 — with expiry tracking
Insurance
Cyber liability policy with renewal date

These object sets didn't overlap. Putting them in the same navigation was the root cause of the confusion. The IA decision followed directly: split the products. VRM for customers. TMH for vendors.

Feedback Patterns

Designing for visibility

Before the redesign, users had no reliable way to know where an assessment stood, what required their attention, or whether an action had succeeded. This generated support tickets — not because the feature was broken, but because users couldn't read the system.

📊

Progress States

Visible lifecycle tracking — assigned → in progress → completed → expired

🗂️

Empty States

Contextual guidance when a section has no content, with clear calls to action

✅

Success Confirmation

In-context confirmation for key actions — inviting a vendor, submitting a response

⚠️

Inline Error Messages

Specific, actionable error copy at the point of failure — not generic alerts

These patterns were built into the Bitsight Design System so they applied consistently across VRM and TMH.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

Run tree testing earlier

I would have pushed to run tree testing on the proposed IA before finalizing the product split — to verify that users could find what they were looking for in the new structure before we committed to building it. We validated with usability studies later, but an earlier tree test would have given us structural confidence sooner and reduced risk at the critical decision point.

Invest more in secondary personas earlier

The unsubscribed users turned out to be the clearest signal of where the platform was failing — but because they had no support relationships, they were harder to reach for research. Their tickets were data; getting them into research sessions would have been more actionable and would have surfaced the feedback pattern gaps much earlier in the process.
